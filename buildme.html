BuildMe is a habit tracker that aims to help users achieve their goals and break bad habits. Its unique features and user-friendly interface make it stand out from other apps.


I. Overview

Have you ever woken up with the motivation or urge to accomplish a new goal? As you embark on this journey you realize you don't know where to start. You begin to realize it's tough to eliminate bad habits and start new ones. Nowadays, lots of people have turned to habit-tracking apps to help them out. Due to factors like technology fatigue many users find themselves going through the motions and have a hard time staying committed to the process of betterment. In this case study, we will explore the effectiveness of BuildMe and the features my team designed to combat the pain points.

II. Context and Challange

Background and Descriotion

The objective was to design and test a professional mobile application for tracking and building habits. The completed application would allow users to stay committed to building and breaking habits. The project was part of a sprint exercise and was to be completed in one month. Despite the timeline, the project would be open for further development and improvements.

Problem statement

When it comes to setting realistic goals, prioritizing our values, and being accountable how can we motivate and entice users to work on themselves? With the increasing prevalence of distractions and device usage, it's important to find a way to strike a balance in our digital habits. As we adapt to the ever-evolving digital landscape, it's crucial that we approach it with mindfulness and intention.

My solution

As my team discovered habit creation is influenced by a combination of intrinsic and extrinsic factors. So our proposed solution is a well-organized application that implements features built around this fact to help users become aware of these factors and also help them stay focused on habits that can help them reach their goals.


Market Research

First, I analyzed competitors like PayPal and Google Pay. I wanted to understand their strengths and weaknesses in a broader sense to find a gap in the market. After conducting market research, I compiled all the information on each competitor and created a S.W.O.T. analysis. This helped me initiate ideas of what users might expect from my app. I also broke down what competitors are doing right to solve their user's problems and ways they are underserving.


User research

I conducted five user interviews, speaking with two participants on March 28th and three on March 29th of 2022. Each participant was excited to help me understand their financial app experiences. Each interviewee had a different career and varying attitudes toward finances. With the data recorded, I had a good amount of information to categorize.

Insights

Social aspects, Financial tools, and Usability.

Wireframes

I created a few sketches by hand and digitally before committing to one that felt like it made the most sense. The rapid prototyping phase was very flexible. Card sorting & site map

I implemented a hybrid card sort blending the closed and open card sorts. Through card sorting, I could see how users interacted with the information being considered for my version of this type of application and validate my hypothesis. Users categorized the content in a way logical to them which helped me consolidate my site map.



Design

Purple will serve as the primary color for PlutoPay due to its psychology and history of having luxurious qualities. The other colors selected will be used for illustrations and complimenting elements like alerts.

Improved wireframes

For my iteration of PlutoPay, I included rewards to entice and make user interactions more meaningful. I will incorporate app-suggested finance challenges and user-generated challenges against contacts similar to challenges on the Apple fitness app. In the badge feature, I designed, I want to include rewards for users to unlock as they use the application.

Conclusion

My original hypothesis still encapsulates what I am trying to solve for users in this sector. Users want a finance application that helps them enjoy handling their finances. People are always looking for a finance app that is convenient and easy to use, but they're aren't many that are fun to use. My proposed solution of introducing gamifying features is viable but there is additional research that needs to be done. Subsequent user testing may change into a form of beta testing for full workflows on a live version of the app. Iterative design is flexible in this aspect because it allows for these changes to take place essentially at any stage.
